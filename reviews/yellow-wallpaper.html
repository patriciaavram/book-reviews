The Yellow Wallpaper

by Charlotte Perkins Gilman

Rating: ★★★★⯪

Summary

Diagnosed by her physician husband with a “temporary nervous depression—a slight hysterical tendency” after the birth of her child, a woman is urged to rest for the summer in an old colonial mansion. Forbidden from doing work of any kind, she spends her days in the house's former nursery, with its barred windows, scratched floor, and peeling yellow wallpaper. In a private journal, the woman records her growing obsession with the “horrid” wallpaper. Its strange pattern mutates in the moonlight, revealing what appears to be a human figure in the design. With nothing else to occupy her mind, the woman resolves to unlock the mystery of the wallpaper. Her quest, however, leads not to the truth, but into the darkest depths of madness. A condemnation of the patriarchy, The Yellow Wallpaper explores with terrifying economy the oppression, grave misunderstanding, and willful dismissal of women in late nineteenth-century society.

My General Thoughts

I've never read a book that describes postpartum depression and the psychosis it leads to, so this was especially touching. This story was short and everything I needed in the moment, although its length didn't take away from the story in itself. If anything, the short length of the story showed how quickly her mental state deteriorated, and how quickly she lost touch with reality. I've actually heard of this phenomenon happening to women who just delivered, because of the lack of sleep, and the mental and physical load of the ordeal. I also loved the way the story was written, it was very immersive and it almost felt that the wallpaper was actually acting on its own at times.

The husband's character was especially frustrating, since he masked his desire to isolate her as a feeling of care, while he was simply trying to limit her freedom. Telling her not to write and that she should just sleep to get better made it seem like he was trying to detach her from the things that bring her joy, to keep her in this indefinite state of depression and loneliness, and this is exactly the kind of behavior from a partner that has the power to throw someone even deeper into a state of depression. When you keep someone away from something that brings them joy, and that allows them to express themselves and explicitely write what they're feeling, of course they're descend into madness, I would too.

A very creepy aspect of this story is how the author wrote about the wallpaper, so much that it seemed to become its own person toward the ending. I wonder if this wallpaper was inspired by one that the author had encountered in real life.

Thoughts on the Characters

  • The Narrator: Seemed like a lovely woman, quite relatable since, I too, would lose my mind if I was isolated like that.
  • The Narrator's Husband: Despicable little man with a cowardly attitude, he manipulates his wife and disguises it as love and care to mold her to his liking.
  • The Wallpaper: Interesting aspect that I would consider a character in the story: holds a few meanings, I can't tell if it's describing a state of psychosis, a descecnt into madness, or if it's a manifestation of the narrator's own mental state.
