Girl Interrupted

by Susanna Kaysen

Rating: ★★★★☆

Summary

In 1967, after a session with a psychiatrist she'd never seen before, eighteen-year-old Susanna Kaysen was put in a taxi and sent to McLean Hospital. She spent most of the next two years on the ward for teenage girls in a psychiatric hospital as renowned for its famous clientele--Sylvia Plath, Robert Lowell, James Taylor, and Ray Charles--as for its progressive methods of treating those who could afford its sanctuary. Kaysen's memoir encompasses horror and razor-edged perception while providing vivid portraits of her fellow patients and their keepers. It is a brilliant evocation of a "parallel universe" set within the kaleidoscopically shifting landscape of the late sixties. "Girl, Interrupted" is a clear-sighted, unflinching document that gives lasting and specific dimension to our definitions of sane and insane, mental illness, and recovery.

My Thoughts

This book was actually refreshing to read. The fact that it's actually a memoir really puts her mental health struggles into perspective, which is an aspect that I found to be a great bonus to the book. Many fictional books about mental illness can be a bit shallow in the sense that the author writes about something they don't necessarily understand or lived through. I had read books such as The Bell Jar, that I enjoyed just the same, that dig deep into how the writer felt about their own mental condition.

I have not seen the movie adaptation of this Girl, Interrupted, but I heard that people like it more than the book because the book feels empty to them. The fact that this book is a memoir written by adult Suzanna, about young Suzanna, means that obviously she has had some time to heal and come to terms with her illness, which can give the book the detached aspect that people dislike. I honestly thought it was very relevant and very realistic. The writing style is very straightforward and simple, which is a good choice for this book since the goal was simply to write about past experiences; it feels like the writer is directly telling the story to the reader, without embellishing, which again was very refreshing.

The choice of that painting was very fitting to describe the struggles of mental illness and being institutionalized, I definitely see the vision and can see why teenage Suzanna latched on so much to it.
