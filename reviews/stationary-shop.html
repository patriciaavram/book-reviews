Letters from the Ginza Shihodo Stationery Shop

by Kenji Ueda, Emily Balistrieri (Translator)

Rating: ★★★☆☆

Summary

Hidden away in a corner of the Ginza neighbourhood is a venerable stationery shop. To venture inside is to find everything your stationery-loving heart desires, from the most delicate paper to fountain pens that fit exactly to the shape of your hand to gorgeously coloured inks. The shop owner intuits your every need, inviting you to take a seat at a small wooden table on the top floor, where you'll find the words flowing, helping you unlock repressed memories, secret longings and your own mysteries. To this shop comes a young company employee, uncertain in his career and needing a connection back to his past; the hostess of an elegant club; the vice-captain of a high-school archery team, an ageing businessman and a formerly homeless sushi chef. With impeccable manners and a warm demeanour, the shop owner helps each of them with more than just their stationery needs.

My Thoughts

I'll be honest, the book was nice and cozy, which is what I was going for in the first place. However, the pacing was a bit slow at times, and I ended up skimming through some of it. It felt like this book was too long for the stories it held. The terminology was hard to understand at times, and I'm not referring to the stationary terms because I'm quite familiar with the different stationary products that exist, but rather the Japanese cultural context was lost on me, but that might be my own fault. The stories of the high-school archery team was the one that felt slow to me, the story of the hostess of the club and of the sushi chef was were my favourites. Overall, was a nice read and cozy, but I feel like it lacked substance at times. I don't have many opinions on it and won't be rereading soon.
