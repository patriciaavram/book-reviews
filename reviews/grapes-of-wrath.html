The Grapes of Wrath

by John Steinbeck

Rating: ★★★★★

Summary

First published in 1939, Steinbeck's Pulitzer Prize-winning epic of the Great Depression chronicles the Dust Bowl migration of the 1930s and tells the story of one Oklahoma farm family, the Joads—driven from their homestead and forced to travel west to the promised land of California. Out of their trials and their repeated collisions against the hard realities of an America divided into Haves and Have-Nots evolves a drama that is intensely human yet majestic in its scale and moral vision, elemental yet plainspoken, tragic but ultimately stirring in its human dignity. A portrait of the conflict between the powerful and the powerless, of one man's fierce reaction to injustice, and of one woman's stoical strength, the novel captures the horrors of the Great Depression and probes into the very nature of equality and justice in America. At once a naturalistic epic, captivity narrative, road novel, and transcendental gospel, Steinbeck's powerful landmark novel is perhaps the most American of American Classics.

My Thoughts

Very difficult and complex read, but incredible story overall. Each character was different and had their own ambitions and path they wanted to follow, but the family had to move as a unit, which really revealed the characters' weaknesses. Granma and Granpa's deaths being only a couple of days apart were absolutely tragic, I can definitely sympathise with Granma's reaction. She was being so brave for the children to not see her suffering, and tried to deal with it on her own but in the end it was too much. The author tried to paint Tom as the hero of this story, but in my opinion the person who held the team together the most and took care of those who suffered was Ma. She is truly the picture of compassion, helping others when possible, feeding them, holding the family together in their hardships. She took care of Rose of Sharon during her pregnancy and most importantly kept her calm when Connie left, and when she gave birth to her baby who was unfortunately stillborn. The amount of suffering Ma went through gave her a kind heart, she did not hesitate to help others regardless of her own family's shortcomings. She supported the family emotionally, and was the backbone of this story in my opinion. Raising Ruthie to be a tough girl was a + in my books; Ma is a strong woman and an example of how well women are able to lead a group. However, I felt that Pa, who is supposed to be the leader of the family, was somewhat useless in times of crisis. He did lead the conversations with Tom and Al, when deciding where to go next for work, but even then, his voice was only as strong as his boys' voices. The preacher coming back was a nice surprise too, and his involvement in the rebellion was shortlived and I wish it wasn't. His death came too soon in my opinion, but it was a very necessary event in the plot, leading Tom to be separated from the group. I don't have much to say about Tom, or Al, they were all doing their duties and working in the fields to feed their families, and it was nice that they both collaborated so well to take care of the truck this whole time. All around, good characters, there wasn't anyone I disliked as they were all going through a very difficult change in their lives and their reactions to everything were valid.

I am not usually one to read historical fiction, even though some of my favourite books are stories set in major historical events. This is a story about a migratory movement, but more importantly, this is a story about a poor family, driven away from their home, forced to start over in a world full of other poor families, competing to find a source of income to keep themselves alive, clashing against rich corporations that take advantage of people just trying to survive. A very relevant read in today's sociopolitical landscape, that just shows the lengths of corporations' greed, and the pay gap between normal people and billionaires. 5 stars, no notes.
