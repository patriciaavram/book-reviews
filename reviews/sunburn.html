Sunburn

by Chloe Michelle Howarth

Rating: ★★★★⯪

Summary

It's the early 1990s, and in the Irish village of Crossmore, Lucy feels out of place. Despite her fierce friendships, she's always felt this way, and the conventional path of marriage and motherhood doesn't appeal to her at all. Not even with handsome and doting Martin, her closest childhood friend. Lucy begins to make sense of herself during a long hot summer, when a spark with her school friend Susannah escalates to an all-consuming infatuation, and, very quickly, to a desperate and devastating love. Fearful of rejection from her small and conservative community, Lucy begins living a double life, hiding the most honest parts of herself in stolen moments with Susannah. But with the end of school and the opportunity to leave Crossmore looming, Lucy must choose between two places, two people and two futures, each as terrifying as the other. But only one can offer her real happiness. Sunburn is an astute and tender portrayal of first love, adolescent anxiety and the realities of growing up in a small town where tradition holds people tightly in its grasp.

My Thoughts

This novel was genuinely one of the most accurate portrayals of a teenager finding themselves that I've ever read. Sometimes we have to fight between telling the truth, living our lives according to who we really are, but on the other side in situations where you have a very traditional family, you have to choose between being yourself or making your family happy. This is especially tough in Lucy's situations, when a lot of people are counting on her to do the 'right' choice and leave with Martin, whereas in her heart, she knows that Susannah is the right one for her. The pressure of having to decide right after school ended did not help her case, as this sort of thing is very difficult to accept if you grew up in a certain environment that is less tolerant, as was portrayed through the differences in Susannah and Lucy's families, the latter being more conservative and not accepting of other sexualities. In contrast, Susannah's family being detached from her but being more modern allowed her the freedom to explore those feelings and relationships more easily caused a clash in her opinions on this topic compared to Lucy's opinions. Something that Susannah perhaps deemed normal, felt to Lucy as the most perverse thing in the world, something that would bring shame upon her family, and therefore would be something to avoid getting into.

I did find myself getting frustrated at Lucy's decisions at times but I ultimately believe that everything led her to learning more about herself, and every decision, whether it is about her relationships, her jobs, her friends, led her to be sure of who she is and what she wants. I don't think I can be upset at her inability to make a clear choice right out of high school, it takes a while to be able to make a clear-headed decision, especially about settling down, beginning a life with someone, and choosing to be yourself or conforming and prioritizing your family's feelings, and she had every right to be confused and scared at both options. Taking a step back to think about it was the right thing to do in that case, but unfortuately it had to be at the expense of Martin and Geraldine's feelings. On another hand, Lucy and Susannah's letter exchanges was some of the most beautiful and tender letter writing I've ever read, and really showcased how entertwined their souls are even after years of not interacting. Susannah was also valid in her actions of leaving and travelling across the world, seeing new things and meeting new people, which I'm sure solidified her feelings toward Lucy and also allowed her to set clear boundaries when things got too overwhelming to handle. I think that in order for both of them to meet again and decide if they really wanted to pursue a life together, they had to go through these much needed experiences, and learn about themselves and their goals. Unfortunately this did cause me a lot of sadness, as I cannot even imagine the pain of finding your person at such a young age and then losing them for years even though they are just out of reach.

The writing and narration style of this novel reminded me of the narration of The Perks of Being a Wallflower, in the sense that it felt like an instant classic, a coming-of-age novel with a story that's rarely told well as well as characters that are diverse and extremely accurately portrayed. The introspective narration style was captivating, and didn't feel boring at any point, even when routinely following Lucy's days in school. Reading about how she felt about her attraction to Susannah was extremely well described and relevant to a lot of teenagers' experiences with self-discovery, including mine. Brilliantly written novel, which is in my opinion a modern-day classic.
