Summary

Innovative and deeply poetic, this landmark work of literary fiction, The Waves, is often regarded as Virginia Woolf's masterpiece. It begins with six children―three boys and three girls―playing in a garden by the sea, and follows their lives as they grow up, experience friendship and love, and grapple with the death of their beloved friend Percival. Instead of describing their outward expressions of grief, Woolf uses a groundbreaking stream of consciousness style to draw her characters from the inside, revealing their inner lives: their aspirations, their triumphs and regrets, their awareness of unity and isolation.

Quite honestly, this was a very beautiful book, there were some aspects of it that clicked for me and others that didn't really resonate, so it was a 3.5/5 stars for me. In terms of quality of writing and of the story, I would rate this a 4 stars. The only thing I would say was missing for me was the lack of action in the plot. However, in this stream of consciousness style of writing, it's understandable that the point isn't to have a very detailed plot, but rather to capture the feelings and thoughts of the characters. In this case, Woolf captured the themes of aging and death beautifully, showing that the thoughts of all the characters converged into a similar perspective as they went through life and experienced complex emotions like grief for their friend Percival.

I had a bit of a hard time staying focused at times, just because I would get lost in trying to understand the writing and what the different analogies and metaphors meant. I found that when I just let myself get lost in the writing instead of trying to analyze it, I was able to enjoy it much more and focus on the story better. It felt like I was trying too hard to enjoy this book, it felt tedious to get through at times. The characters were all very unique and I loved how they started differentiating more and more as they got older, but with a similar view on life topics. I also really liked how the story was framed by the beginning and ending with the same scene of the children playing in the garden, it really made the story into a full circle moment and was extremely touching.

Overall, I will definitely need to reread this to fully grasp the story, but I can see why it's considered a classic. I feel I was either not experienced enough in reading this stream of consciousness style, or that this type of prose just isn't for me, so I couldn't fully understand it. I don't feel like I was 100% immersed in the story, which made it hard for me to fully enjoy it.
