Giovanni's Room

by James Baldwin

Rating: ★★★★⯪

Summary

Set in the contemporary Paris of American expatraites, liasons, and violence, a young man finds himself caught between desire and conventional morality. James Baldwin's brilliant narrative delves into the mystery of loving with a sharp, probing imagination, and he creates a moving, highly controversial story of death and passion that reveals the unspoken complexities of the heart.

My Thoughts

This novel was at once extremely frustrating and heartbreaking. Compared to my most recently read novel, which was Sunburn by Chloe Michelle Howarth, they both represented the trials and tribulations of learning and accepting who you are and who you are attracted to and want to build a life with. As in Lucy's case, I felt that David was ashamed and dismissive of his true nature, often depicting his love/attraction for Giovanni as revolting, ugly and flawed. They both often wonder how will a relationship with a person of the same gender could ever work out, but I felt that Lucy's doubts were almost always internalized, as opposed to David's thoughts that exploded in defense when Giovanni accused him of leaving him. However, I'd say the main differences between those two novels were the plot richness and the narration. While Sunburn focuses a lot more on the reality of being a teenage girl realizing she doesn't conform to traditional norms, as well as doing a deep dive into that girl's brain throughout the end of high-school and moving out period, Giovanni's room had a more action-packed plot. It diverged from Sunburn in the sense that it didn't feel like I was just reading David's diary, but instead that I was actually going through the events with him, and in that aspect the book felt more impersonal. In terms of my enjoyment of it though, I connected more with this narration that has some key events sprinkled into it, than with a narration that made me feel like I was reading about someone's daily life and thoughts. Also need to mention that I did not expect Giovanni to have actually committed that crime he was found guilty of, interesting plot twist that really unravelled David's entire thread of feelings toward him and showcased his indifference to Hella.

One thing that knocked a few points off the rating scale for me was the almost constant misogynistic opinions coming from Giovanni and David. You'd think they would be more sensitive given their different experiences than the norm in society, but at the end of the day they were intolerant. David really annoyed me in talking about women this way but then leaving Giovanni to settle down with Hella instead. Pick a side where you don't need to pretend to be someone else. However, I did appreciate how well Baldwin writes women in this book, especially Hella. Usually, reading older novels feels like the women are stereotyped into diffent categories that don't really reflect how they are in real life, but in this book he really nailed it. Hella deciding to do what's better for herself and leaving the country and David entirely once she learned the truth about him wasn't exactly what I was expecting given the era that this novel was writte in, but I was pleasantly surprised with his depiction of her. From a personal standpoint, I'm also glad that she had her own strong opinions and that she had the drive to travel by herself to figure out what she wants, it was what she needed in order to decide she wanted to settle with David.

Overall, incredible novel, incredible portrayal of the complexity of dealing with feelings that are out of the norm and that wouldn't be accepted and taken lightly in a discriminative society. Very enjoyable read.
